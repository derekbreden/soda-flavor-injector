# Dock Mounting Strategies — Under-Sink Placement Research

Where the dock lives in the cabinet determines almost everything about the cartridge change experience: how far the user reaches, whether they can see what they're doing, how tubes and wires route to the main body, and whether a release lever is even practical without pulling something out of the cabinet first.

This document surveys real under-sink products, analyzes every plausible mounting location, and scores them against each other. The goal is to choose a dock position before committing to the dock's mechanical design.

**Cartridge reference dimensions:** ~140 x 90 x 100mm (W x H x D), ~2.1 lbs / 940g
**Dock estimate:** ~500g (rails, fittings, back plate, pogo pins)
**Combined dock + cartridge:** ~1.5 kg / 3.3 lbs

---

## 1. Survey of Under-Sink Mounting Patterns

### 1a. Water Filtration Systems (Closest Analog)

Water filters are the best prior art because they share the same fundamental interaction: a permanently mounted head/bracket receives a replaceable cartridge, connected to house water with 1/4" tubing.

**Pentair Everpure H-300**
- Head bracket screws to the **back wall or side wall** of the cabinet
- Cartridge hangs **vertically downward** from the head
- Requires **22 inches minimum vertical clearance** beneath the countertop
- Needs **2.5 inches clearance below the cartridge** for removal (twist off downward)
- Head dimensions: roughly 4" wide x 3" deep x 3" tall
- Cartridge: ~3.5" diameter x 13" long, hangs straight down
- Mounting hardware: 2 wood screws into cabinet wall
- Replacement motion: quarter-turn twist, pull straight down
- Source: Pentair installation manual, efilters.net installation guide

**3M Aqua-Pure AP Easy Complete**
- Bracket with quick-connect fittings, screws to **back wall**
- Cartridge is **vertical, hanging down** from the head assembly
- Included hardware: mounting screws, bracket is integral to head assembly
- Replacement: twist and pull, tool-free
- The attached mounting bracket + quick-connect fittings make it a single-point wall mount
- Source: 3M product page, DrinkingWellCo

**GE GXK140TNN / GXRLQR Series**
- "Twist and Lock" design — cartridge twists into head
- Head bracket mounts to **back wall** with screws
- Vertical orientation, cartridge hangs down
- Replacement every 6 months, tool-free twist
- Source: GE Appliances, Home Depot listings

**DuPont QuickTwist (WFQT130005)**
- Built-in mounting bracket with designer cover
- Push-on tube fittings
- Built-in shutoff valve (no need to turn off water for cartridge swap)
- Mounts to **back wall** with included hardware
- Source: Amazon, FiltersFirst

**Waterdrop TSU**
- Overall system dimensions: 4.68" W x 13.97" H x 14.81" L
- Mounts to **back wall** — compact enough to fit beside a garbage disposal
- Source: WaterdropFilter.com, Home Depot

**Pattern Summary — Water Filters:**

| Brand | Mount Surface | Orientation | Cartridge Direction | Clearance Needed |
|-------|--------------|-------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Everpure H-300 | Back/side wall | Vertical | Hangs down | 22" vertical |
| 3M Aqua-Pure | Back wall | Vertical | Hangs down | ~18" vertical |
| GE Twist & Lock | Back wall | Vertical | Hangs down | ~15" vertical |
| DuPont QuickTwist | Back wall | Vertical | Hangs down | ~15" vertical |
| Waterdrop TSU | Back wall | Vertical | Hangs down | ~14" vertical |

**Key insight:** Every major consumer water filter mounts to the **back wall** with the cartridge **hanging vertically downward**. The head is at the top (near the countertop underside), and the cartridge drops below. Replacement is always a twist-and-pull-down motion. None mount to the ceiling (countertop underside) directly, though the head is typically placed high on the wall, which achieves a similar effect. The vertical-hang pattern means gravity assists removal and the cartridge naturally drains after disconnection.

### 1b. Garbage Disposals

- Mount **to the sink drain from above** — not relocatable
- Typical dimensions: 6-9" diameter x 10-15" tall
- Consume the center of the under-sink space directly below the drain
- Leave two usable zones on either side, each roughly 10-14" wide
- Relevant because they define the "no-go zone" in the center of the cabinet

### 1c. Instant Hot Water Dispensers

- **InSinkErator** tanks mount to the **back wall or side wall** with 2 wood screws
- Tank positioned **vertically**, 3" minimum above cabinet floor
- Must be within 16" of faucet water lines and 30" of an electrical outlet
- Tank is roughly 4" diameter x 10-12" tall
- Wall clearance: 0.4" from back wall for air circulation
- Source: InSinkErator installation guide

### 1d. Soap Dispensers and Small Accessories

- Soap dispensers mount through the sink deck (top-down), tank hangs below
- Small items (sponge caddies, towel hooks) use adhesive or single screws on door or wall
- Not directly relevant, but shows that door-mount is common for lightweight items

### 1e. Pull-Out Organizers

- Under-sink pull-out organizers use **full-extension ball-bearing drawer slides**
- Typical slide specs: 12-14" length, 100 lb capacity, 1/2" wide x 1-7/8" tall profile
- Slides mount to the cabinet side walls or a frame
- Pull-out trash cans, cutting boards, and shelf organizers all use this hardware
- Standard slides cost $8-20 per pair for 100 lb capacity
- Rev-A-Shelf and similar brands sell complete under-sink pull-out kits
- Source: Rockler, Hardware Resources, Home Depot

### 2a. Back Wall, Mid-Height

The dock screws to the back wall of the cabinet, roughly 12-18" above the floor. The cartridge faces forward (toward the user) and slides in horizontally.

```
    SIDE VIEW (cross-section)

    countertop
    ══════════════════════════════
    │                            │
    │  back   ┌────────┐        │
    │  wall   │  DOCK  │←cart.  │ ← 12-18" above floor
    │         │        │  slides│
    │         └────────┘  in    │
    │                            │
    ══════════════════════════════
    floor
              ├── ~6" ─►│← ~16" to front
```

**Reach depth:** Cabinet is ~22" deep. Dock back plate is against the wall. Dock body ~25mm deep. Cartridge ~100mm deep. The cartridge's front face sits roughly 5" (125mm) from the back wall. The user reaches **~17 inches** to touch the release lever, or **~22 inches** to reach the back of the dock. This is a full arm's-length reach while crouching.

**Sight lines:** User must crouch and look horizontally into the cabinet. The dock is visible if there's nothing in front of it, but the mating face is in shadow (facing toward the user but recessed).

**Insertion motion:** Reach in, align cartridge with guide rails, push straight toward the back wall. Requires arm extension and some fumbling in the dark.

**Plumbing interference:** Supply lines (hot/cold shutoff valves) typically run vertically on the back wall, 2-4" from the wall surface. The dock must be positioned to avoid these — usually offset to one side. This is a real constraint that varies by installation.

**Tube routing:** Short, direct runs from dock fittings down to the main body (which sits on the cabinet floor or nearby). Tubes run straight down from the dock.

**Lever access:** The lever is on the cartridge's front face, facing the user. Accessible, but 17"+ away. Operating a lever at arm's length while crouched is awkward.

**Verdict:** This is the "obvious" mounting position and how most water filters work (except they use a vertical hang, not horizontal slide-in). For a horizontal slide-in cartridge, the reach depth is the primary problem. Water filters solve this by making the replacement motion a simple twist — no lever to operate at depth.

### 2b. Side Wall

The dock screws to the left or right side panel of the cabinet. The cartridge slides in from the side (left-to-right or right-to-left).

```
    TOP VIEW (looking down)

    ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
    │ back wall                       │
    │                                 │
    │                    ┌──────────┐ │
    │                    │   DOCK   │ │ ← right side wall
    │           cart. ──►│          │ │
    │          slides    │          │ │
    │          in        └──────────┘ │
    │                                 │
    │ front (door opening)            │
    └─────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Reach depth:** The reach is now across the cabinet **width**, not depth. For a 36" cabinet with a center drain taking ~8", each side zone is 10-14" wide. The dock on the far side of a zone is only **10-14 inches** from the nearest edge — much shorter reach than back-wall mounting. If mounted on the near side wall (closest to the cabinet opening), reach could be as short as **6-8 inches**.

**Sight lines:** The dock is perpendicular to the user's line of sight. The mating face is visible when the user leans to look at the side wall. Better than back-wall for seeing the alignment.

**Insertion motion:** Reach in, push cartridge sideways. Less natural than pushing straight back, but the short reach compensates. The motion is more like sliding a book onto a shelf.

**Plumbing interference:** Side walls are typically clear of plumbing (supply lines are on the back wall). The P-trap may extend to one side but usually stays centered.

**Tube routing:** Tubes must route from the side wall dock across to wherever the main body sits. Longer horizontal runs, but still manageable with 1/4" tubing.

**Lever access:** The lever faces the user (or faces sideways depending on orientation). Shorter reach means easier lever operation.

**Structural note:** Side walls on standard kitchen cabinets are typically 1/2" to 3/4" plywood or particle board — adequate for screws holding 3.3 lbs. However, if the dock is on a shared wall between cabinets, the material may be thinner (3/8" particle board in cheaper cabinets).

**Verdict:** Significantly more accessible than back-wall mounting. The short reach makes lever operation practical. Downsides: reduces usable width in that zone, and tube routing is slightly longer.

### 2c. Cabinet Ceiling (Underside of Countertop)

The dock hangs from the underside of the countertop. The cartridge either pushes UP into the dock or hangs DOWN from it.

```
    SIDE VIEW (cross-section)

    countertop
    ══════════════╦══════════════
    │             ║ DOCK         │
    │             ║ ┌──────┐     │
    │             ║ │ cart │     │ ← cartridge hangs from dock
    │             ║ │      │     │
    │             ║ └──────┘     │
    │             ╚══════════    │
    │                            │
    │         (floor space       │
    │          preserved)        │
    ══════════════════════════════
    floor
```

**This is how most water filters work**, just indirectly — the head bracket mounts high on the back wall and the cartridge hangs down. Mounting directly to the countertop underside achieves the same effect more directly.

**Reach:** The dock is near the top of the cabinet. The user reaches UP, not IN. For cartridge change: reach up, operate release, lower the cartridge. The countertop underside is ~28" above the floor, so the bottom of the cartridge hangs at roughly 22-24" — about waist height when crouching.

**Insertion motion — cartridge hangs DOWN from dock:**
- Insert: push cartridge up into dock, engage fittings
- Remove: release mechanism, cartridge drops into user's hand
- Gravity assists removal (cartridge's own weight pulls it out once released)
- Gravity opposes insertion (user must push up and hold while engaging)
- Water drains out of tubes when cartridge is removed (down = good for draining)

**Insertion motion — cartridge pushes UP into dock:**
- Similar to above but the cartridge stays above the dock
- Less natural; the user can't see the mating face (it faces up, hidden by the cartridge)

**Floor space:** Maximum floor space preserved. Nothing on the floor, walls, or in the way.

**Sight lines:** The user cannot see the mating face (it faces down or up, hidden). Insertion is by feel + guide rails. This is how water filters work — the twist-lock mechanism provides tactile confirmation without requiring sight.

**Structural concerns:** The countertop underside is typically:
- Granite/quartz: extremely strong, but requires special anchors (toggle bolts through plywood subtop)
- Laminate on particle board: the subtop is 3/4" particle board — can hold screws, but pulling force must be considered
- Solid wood butcher block: excellent screw holding

For a 1.5 kg load plus insertion force (~20N = ~4.5 lbs push force), the total peak load on the ceiling mount is roughly 8 lbs. A pair of #8 wood screws into 3/4" plywood or particle board can hold 50+ lbs in withdrawal, so this is well within limits.

**Tube routing:** Tubes run straight down from the dock to the main body on the floor. Short, clean, gravity-assisted drainage.

**Lever access:** The lever would be on the cartridge, which hangs at roughly waist/chest height when crouching. Accessible, but the user operates it while looking up. For our horizontal slide-in design, this changes to a vertical slide-up design, which changes the release plate mechanics.

**Design impact:** Ceiling mounting implies the cartridge inserts **vertically** (pushed up), not horizontally. This fundamentally changes the mating face orientation and lever direction from the current horizontal slide-in assumption. However, if we mounted the dock upside-down on the ceiling with horizontal rails, the cartridge could still slide in horizontally at head-height — but then the dock protrudes far down from the ceiling, consuming the vertical space anyway.

**Verdict:** Excellent space efficiency and good ergonomics for a vertical-insert design. Requires rethinking the insertion axis from horizontal to vertical. The water filter industry has proven this pattern works at scale.

### 2d. Cabinet Floor

The dock sits on the cabinet floor. The cartridge drops in from above.

```
    SIDE VIEW (cross-section)

    countertop
    ══════════════════════════════
    │                            │
    │                            │
    │                            │
    │                            │
    │                            │
    │         ┌──────────┐       │
    │         │   cart    │←drop │
    │         │          │  in   │
    ══════════╧══════════╧═══════
    floor     └── DOCK ──┘
```

**Reach:** Minimal — the dock is right there at the bottom of the cabinet when the door opens. No crouching into the cabinet required; the user just bends down.

**Insertion motion:** Drop cartridge straight down into dock cradle. Most natural motion — gravity assists. Removal: lift straight up.

**Sight lines:** Excellent — the user looks down at the dock, can see the mating face and alignment features clearly.

**Lever access:** Right there, fully visible and accessible.

**Water exposure:** This is the critical problem. The cabinet floor is the lowest point, where:
- Condensation from cold water pipes drips
- Small leaks from supply connections pool
- Spills from cleaning products collect
- Garbage disposal leaks accumulate

Even small amounts of standing water would contact the dock's electrical connections (pogo pins). A raised platform (rubber feet, standoffs) mitigates this partially, but the fundamental proximity to water remains.

**Stability:** The dock could be secured with screws, adhesive-backed velcro, or just rubber feet (gravity keeps it in place). Very simple "installation."

**Tube routing:** Tubes are at floor level and must route across the floor to the main body. Horizontal runs on the floor are trip/crush hazards and get in the way of other items stored under the sink.

**Vibration:** The cabinet floor is the worst surface for pump vibration — it acts as a sounding board, amplifying noise through the cabinet and countertop. Rubber isolation feet help but don't eliminate the issue.

**Verdict:** Best accessibility, worst water safety. Would work if the dock were raised on a platform and the cabinet floor were reliably dry. But "reliably dry under-sink cabinet floor" is an oxymoron for many kitchens.

### 2e. Pull-Out Shelf/Tray

The dock is permanently mounted on a pull-out tray that rides on drawer slides. The user pulls the tray forward, bringing the dock and cartridge into the open.

```
    TOP VIEW — tray retracted

    ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
    │ back wall                       │
    │                                 │
    │  ┌─────────────────────────┐    │
    │  │  TRAY  ┌──────┐        │    │
    │  │        │ DOCK │        │    │ ← tray in cabinet
    │  │        │+cart │        │    │
    │  │        └──────┘        │    │
    │  └─────────────────────────┘    │
    │                                 │
    │ front (door opening)            │
    └─────────────────────────────────┘

    TOP VIEW — tray extended

    ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
    │ back wall                       │
    │                                 │
    │  ┌──────────────────────────────┼──────────────────┐
    │  │  TRAY  ┌──────┐             │                   │
    │  │        │ DOCK │             │  ← tray pulled    │
    │  │        │+cart │             │    out to front    │
    │  │        └──────┘             │                   │
    │  └──────────────────────────────┼──────────────────┘
    │                                 │
    │ front (door opening)            │
    └─────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Reach:** Effectively zero. The tray brings the dock to the user. The dock is fully outside the cabinet and at whatever height the tray is mounted. This **completely solves the reach problem**.

**Sight lines:** Perfect — the dock is in the open, fully visible, well-lit.

**Lever access:** Perfect — no reaching, no crouching into the cabinet.

**Drawer slide specifications:**
- 12" full-extension ball-bearing slides: 100 lb capacity standard, $8-20/pair
- 14" slides also common, slightly more travel
- Profile: 1/2" wide x 1-7/8" tall per slide
- Mounting: screws into side walls of cabinet (plywood/particle board)
- Full extension means the tray extends to 100% of its depth outside the cabinet
- Soft-close option available for ~$5 more per pair

**Weight analysis:**
- Dock: ~500g
- Cartridge: ~940g
- Tray (3/4" plywood, ~10" x 14"): ~400g
- Slides (pair): ~300g
- **Total moving weight: ~2.1 kg / 4.7 lbs**
- 100 lb rated slides handle this with massive safety margin (~20:1)

**Tube routing — the critical challenge:**
The dock moves ~12-14" forward when the tray extends. All 4 water tubes (1/4" OD) and the electrical wiring must accommodate this motion.

1/4" polyethylene tubing minimum bend radius: ~19mm (3/4") for cold bends. With 14" of travel, the tubes need a **service loop** — extra coiled length that unfurls as the tray extends and coils back as it retracts.

```
    SIDE VIEW — tray retracted (tubes coiled)

    ┌──────────────────────────┐
    │  back wall               │
    │    │                     │
    │    │ tubes coil here     │
    │    │  ╭───╮              │
    │    │  │   │              │
    │    └──╯   ╰──── DOCK    │
    │              on tray     │
    └──────────────────────────┘

    SIDE VIEW — tray extended (tubes straightened)

    ┌──────────────────────────┐───────────────┐
    │  back wall               │               │
    │    │                     │               │
    │    │ tubes now straight   │               │
    │    └─────────────────────┼──── DOCK      │
    │                          │    on tray    │
    └──────────────────────────┘───────────────┘
```

Required extra tube length: ~14" (350mm) per tube. With 4 tubes at 1/4" OD, the coiled bundle is manageable — 4 tubes side by side occupy ~1" (25mm) width, and a 350mm service loop at 20mm bend radius makes roughly 2-3 loose coils.

**Alternative: cable chain / energy chain.** Plastic cable chains (igus or similar, ~$10-15 for a 15" length) provide controlled bending for both tubes and wires. These are proven in CNC machines, 3D printers, and industrial applications. A 15x20mm cable chain can hold 4 x 1/4" tubes plus a pair of 18 AWG wires easily.

**Wire routing:** Same service loop or cable chain approach. 12V wires are low voltage, flexible, and tolerate repeated flexing well. Stranded 18 AWG silicone-jacketed wire is ideal for repeated bending.

**Installation complexity:** Moderate. The user must:
1. Mount two slides to the cabinet side walls (4-6 screws per slide)
2. Mount the tray to the slides
3. Mount the dock to the tray
4. Route tubes and wires with sufficient service loop

This is more work than a simple wall bracket but well within DIY capability. Pre-assembled tray + slides + dock would reduce this to mounting the slides only.

**Cost of additional hardware:**
- Drawer slides (12" full extension, 100 lb): ~$12-18/pair
- Tray material (plywood or plastic): ~$5-10
- Cable chain (optional): ~$10-15
- **Total additional: ~$25-45**

**Verdict:** The pull-out tray is the accessibility gold standard. It solves every ergonomic problem simultaneously. The cost is moderate additional hardware, slightly more complex installation, and tube/wire management for the moving joint. The tube routing is the primary engineering challenge but is well-solved by service loops or cable chains.

### 2f. Swing-Out Bracket

The dock is on a hinged bracket that swings out from the cabinet wall (like a swing-out spice rack or blind corner organizer).

```
    TOP VIEW — bracket closed

    ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
    │ back wall                       │
    │                                 │
    │  ┌──┐                           │
    │  │D ├── bracket (folded in)     │
    │  └──┘                           │
    │                                 │
    │ front                           │
    └─────────────────────────────────┘

    TOP VIEW — bracket swung out

    ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
    │ back wall                       │
    │                                 │
    │  ╔══╗                           │
    │  ║  ╠═══════╗                   │
    │  ╚══╝ hinge ║ DOCK             ←── dock swings out
    │             ║ + cart            │    to front
    │             ╚═══════           │
    │ front                           │
    └─────────────────────────────────┘
```

**Reach:** Very good when swung out — the dock is near the cabinet opening.

**Sight lines:** Good when swung out.

**Mechanical complexity:** Significantly higher than a pull-out tray:
- Needs a piano hinge or heavy-duty pivot rated for the load + vibration
- The swing arc must clear the P-trap, supply lines, and other obstacles
- Counterbalance may be needed to keep the bracket from slamming open/closed
- The arc motion means tubes experience both extension and bending through a curved path

**Tube routing:** Harder than pull-out. The tubes must accommodate an arc, not a straight extension. A cable chain works but must bend in a curve, not just a straight line. Service loops work but need more length to accommodate the arc.

**Rev-A-Shelf and similar swing-out organizers** exist for blind corners and pantries:
- Use industrial piano hinges
- Rated for 20-50 lbs typically
- Cost: $40-100+ for the swing mechanism alone
- Designed for shelves, not precision-mounted equipment

**Vibration concern:** A hinged bracket has more flex than a slide-mounted tray. Pump vibration could cause the bracket to oscillate, especially if the hinge has any play.

**Verdict:** More complex than a pull-out tray with no clear advantage. The arc motion complicates tube routing, the hinge adds flex/vibration concerns, and the hardware costs more. Pull-out tray is strictly better for this application.

### 2g. Door-Mounted

The dock mounts to the inside of the cabinet door. When the door opens, the dock is right there.

```
    SIDE VIEW — door closed

    ┌──────────────────────────┐
    │ back wall                │
    │                          │
    │                          │
    │                          │
    │                          │
    │              DOCK + cart │ ← mounted on door
    └══════════════════════════┘
                  door (closed)

    SIDE VIEW — door open

    ┌──────────────────────────┐
    │ back wall                │
    │                          │       ┌──────┐
    │                          │       │ DOCK │
    │                          │       │+cart │
    │                          │       └──────┘
    └──────────────────────────┘       door (open)
```

**Reach:** The best possible — the dock is literally on the door. When the door opens, the dock is at arm's length, fully visible, perfectly accessible. No reaching into the cabinet at all.

**Sight lines:** Perfect — the dock faces the user when the door is open.

**Weight on door hinges:**
- Standard cabinet hinges (Blum-style European cup hinges, 2 per door): rated for **10-20 lbs per hinge**, or **20-40 lbs total** for a standard door
- Dock + cartridge: 1.5 kg / 3.3 lbs
- Door itself: typically 3-5 lbs (particle board + thermofoil/wood veneer)
- **Total door weight: 6.3-8.3 lbs** — well within hinge ratings

However, the weight is **cantilevered** far from the hinges. A 3.3 lb load mounted 10" from the hinge creates a torque of 33 in-lbs. Standard Blum hinges are rated for this but will experience more wear over time.

**Vibration during door operation:** Opening and closing the door creates acceleration forces on the dock and cartridge. At 3.3 lbs, the inertial forces from a normal door swing are small, but repeated slamming could loosen screws over time. Soft-close hinges mitigate this.

**Door swing clearance:** The dock + cartridge protrude ~125mm (5") from the door's inner surface. The door must be able to close with the dock mounted. In a typical face-frame cabinet, there's ~1/2" clearance between the door inner surface and the cabinet opening. The dock would protrude 5" into the cabinet interior — this must not hit the back wall, P-trap, or other obstructions when the door closes.

For a 22" deep cabinet with a 5" dock protrusion, there's ~17" of clearance between the dock and the back wall when the door is closed. This is fine for clearance.

**Tube routing — the critical problem:**
The tubes and wires must bridge from the moving door to the fixed cabinet. Every time the door opens, the tubes flex through ~110 degrees of arc (most cabinet doors open to about 110-120 degrees).

Required: flexible service loop with enough slack for ~110 degrees of door travel. The hinge point is at the door edge; the tube attachment point is 10-15" from the hinge. The arc length at 12" radius over 110 degrees is ~23 inches of travel. The tubes need a service loop that can accommodate this without kinking.

This is a harder routing problem than the pull-out tray because:
1. The travel distance is longer (~23" arc vs ~14" linear)
2. The motion is rotational, not linear
3. The tubes must not pinch between the door and the cabinet frame as the door closes
4. The flex point is concentrated at the hinge area

**Practical solutions:**
- Spiral cable wrap through the hinge area (common for refrigerator door wiring)
- Flexible conduit routed through a hinged tube guide
- Quick-disconnect fittings at the door (tubes detach when the door opens beyond a certain point) — but this defeats the purpose of permanent connections

**Mounting to the door:**
Standard cabinet doors are 5/8" to 3/4" thick, particle board or MDF core with veneer/thermofoil face. Screws into this material:
- #8 x 3/4" wood screws: adequate for 3.3 lbs static load
- Particle board screw withdrawal resistance: approximately 150-190 N (~34-43 lbs) per screw in face grain at typical densities
- 4 screws provide >100 lbs holding force — far more than needed
- But repeated vibration and door slamming can work screws loose in particle board over time

**Verdict:** Maximum accessibility but the tube routing through the door hinge is a serious engineering challenge. The tubes and wires must flex through 110+ degrees thousands of times without kinking or fatiguing. This is solvable (refrigerators do it for water/ice lines) but adds significant complexity and potential failure points. Door weight and hinge ratings are not a concern.

## 3. Tube Routing Constraints

### 3a. Tubing Specifications

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Tube OD | 1/4" (6.35mm) |
| Tube material | Polyethylene or nylon (hard) |
| Minimum cold bend radius | ~19mm (3/4") for PE, ~25mm (1") for nylon |
| Minimum heat-formed bend radius | ~12mm (1/2") for nylon |
| Number of tubes | 4 (2 pumps x inlet + outlet) |
| Operating pressure | Low (<30 PSI at point of use) |

### 3b. Tube Length by Mounting Location

| Mounting Location | Tube Run (dock to main body) | Notes |
|-------------------|------------------------------|-------|
| Back wall, mid-height | 12-24" (30-60cm) | Straight down + horizontal to main body |
| Side wall | 18-30" (45-75cm) | Horizontal across cabinet + down |
| Ceiling | 12-24" (30-60cm) | Straight down to main body on floor |
| Floor | 6-18" (15-45cm) | Horizontal along floor |
| Pull-out tray | 24-36" (60-90cm) | Includes ~14" service loop for travel |
| Swing-out bracket | 30-48" (75-120cm) | Includes arc service loop |
| Door-mounted | 36-60" (90-150cm) | Includes ~23" arc service loop through hinge |

### 3c. Kink Risk Assessment

**Static mounts (back wall, side wall, ceiling, floor):** Low kink risk. Tubes are routed once during installation and don't move. Proper routing with appropriate bend radii eliminates kink risk.

**Pull-out tray:** Moderate kink risk. The service loop must unfurl and refurl smoothly. A cable chain eliminates this risk entirely by controlling the bend geometry. Without a cable chain, the tubes must be loosely bundled (zip ties every 4-6 inches) to prevent individual tubes from catching.

**Swing-out bracket:** Moderate-high kink risk. The arc motion can cause tubes to twist as well as bend, especially if the tube bundle doesn't rotate cleanly around the pivot.

**Door-mounted:** High kink risk. The 110-degree arc concentrated at the hinge point creates extreme bending. The tubes must be routed through the hinge area with a controlled bend mechanism, or they will kink within weeks of use.

### 3d. Cable Chain Option for Moving Mounts

A plastic energy chain (igus E2 micro series or similar):
- Inner dimensions: 15 x 20mm (holds 4 x 1/4" tubes + wires easily)
- Minimum bend radius: 28mm (1.1") — suitable for 1/4" PE tubing
- Cost: ~$10-15 for a 400mm (16") length
- Rated for millions of cycles
- Proven in CNC machines, 3D printers, and industrial robotics

For the pull-out tray, the cable chain mounts with one end fixed to the cabinet back wall and the other end to the tray. As the tray extends, the chain unfolds in a smooth U-shape.

## 4. Electrical Routing Constraints

The dock needs 3 wires from the main body: ground, motor A 12V, motor B 12V.

### 4a. Wire Specifications

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Wire gauge | 18 AWG (handles 16A, motors draw ~1-2A each) |
| Conductor type | Stranded copper (for flexibility) |
| Insulation | Silicone jacket (flexible, heat resistant) |
| Number of conductors | 3 |
| Total bundle diameter | ~8-10mm for 3x 18 AWG |

### 4b. Routing by Mounting Location

**Static mounts (back wall, side wall, ceiling, floor):**
- Wire once during installation, zip-tie to cabinet wall
- Use a 3-pin quick-disconnect connector at the dock for clean installation/removal
- Connector options: JST SM 3-pin ($2-3), Molex MiniFit Jr ($3-5), automotive weatherproof ($5-8)
- No flex requirements; any wire type works

**Pull-out tray:**
- Wires go through the same cable chain as the tubes
- Silicone-jacketed stranded wire handles the repeated flexing
- Alternatively: coiled/retractable cord (like a telephone handset cord) — cheap, self-managing
- A spiral cable (~$3-5) designed for repeated extension/retraction is the simplest solution

**Swing-out / door-mounted:**
- Same approach as pull-out but with rotational flex
- Spiral cable through the hinge area
- Quick-disconnect at the door is another option — but the user would need to connect/disconnect wires every time they want to change the cartridge, which defeats the plug-and-play goal

## 6. The "Water Filter" Pattern Deep Dive

### Why Do All Water Filters Use the Same Pattern?

Every major consumer under-sink water filter uses the same approach:
1. **Head/bracket screwed to the back wall**, positioned high (near countertop)
2. **Cartridge hangs vertically downward** from the head
3. **Replacement: twist and pull down** — one motion, one hand, tool-free

Why this specific pattern?

**Gravity assists removal:** When the cartridge is released, gravity pulls it down and out. The user doesn't need to hold and pull simultaneously.

**Gravity assists drainage:** A vertically hanging cartridge drains water downward when removed. No trapped water sloshes out sideways.

**Clearance is natural:** The space below the filter head is empty (it's just cabinet air space). No obstruction below means the cartridge can be extracted straight down.

**Reach is acceptable:** The head is on the wall, ~6-8" from the front of the cabinet. The user reaches ~6-8" horizontally to grip the cartridge, then pulls straight down. The total reach is short because the motion is vertical, not horizontal.

**Twist-lock needs no sight lines:** A quarter-turn twist is tactile — the user doesn't need to see the mating face. The twist either clicks or it doesn't. This works in the dark recesses of a cabinet.

### How Our Design Differs

Our cartridge uses **horizontal slide-in** with a **lever release**, which differs from the water filter pattern in important ways:

| Property | Water Filter Pattern | Our Design |
|----------|---------------------|------------|
| Insertion axis | Vertical (up/down) | Horizontal (front/back) |
| Release mechanism | Twist-lock (tactile, blind-friendly) | Lever (needs hand access, preferably visible) |
| Gravity role | Assists removal | Neutral (horizontal = no gravity assist) |
| Drainage | Natural (downward) | Must be managed (horizontal = water trapped in tubes) |
| Sight lines | Not needed (twist is tactile) | Helpful (lever position, alignment) |

**Implication:** The water filter pattern is optimized for a vertical axis and twist-lock release. Our horizontal slide-in + lever design has different ergonomic constraints. The mounting location matters MORE for our design because the user needs to see and operate a lever, not just feel a twist.

### Could We Switch to Vertical Insert?

Switching to vertical insertion would align with the water filter pattern and open up ceiling mounting. Trade-offs:

**Pros of vertical (ceiling-mount, cartridge hangs down):**
- Proven by entire water filter industry
- Gravity assists removal
- Short reach (vertical, not horizontal)
- Floor space preserved

**Cons of vertical:**
- Release plate and lever mechanism designed for horizontal slide — would need redesign
- John Guest fittings are designed for axial tube insertion, which works in both orientations, but the dock would need fittings oriented vertically
- The cartridge would need a handle or grip surface on the bottom for the user to pull down
- Pump orientation changes: motor axis becomes vertical, which can affect pump lifespan (bearings, lubrication)

This is a significant design pivot that would need separate analysis. For now, this document evaluates mounting locations for the current horizontal slide-in design.

### Scoring Rationale

**Pull-out tray (121, 1st):** Wins on the two highest-weighted criteria: accessibility and sight lines, both perfect 5s. Lever compatibility is also a 5. The tube routing concern (3) is real but well-solved by cable chains. Installation is the main weakness — more effort than a simple bracket.

**Side wall (119, 2nd):** Strong all-around with no critical weaknesses. Short reach, decent sight lines, simple installation. The second-best option if the pull-out tray is deemed too complex. Works well for a horizontal slide-in design because the insertion axis is perpendicular to the user's natural reach direction.

**Door-mounted (113, 3rd):** Perfect accessibility and sight lines, but the tube routing through the door hinge (score of 1) is a near-deal-breaker. Would require significant engineering to make reliable.

**Floor and ceiling (109, tied 4th):** Floor wins on accessibility but loses badly on water safety and vibration. Ceiling wins on space efficiency and water safety but loses on accessibility and lever compatibility (hard to operate a lever overhead).

**Back wall (103, 6th):** The "obvious" choice that turns out to be mediocre. The 17"+ reach to operate a lever is the fundamental problem. This is why water filters use a twist-lock instead of a lever — the motion can be performed at arm's length by feel.

**Swing-out (97, 7th):** All the complexity of a moving mount with no clear advantage over the pull-out tray. More expensive hardware, harder tube routing, and vibration concerns from the hinge.

## 8. Recommendations

### Primary Recommendation: Side Wall Mount

For the first prototype, **mount the dock to the side wall** of the cabinet.

Rationale:
- Short reach (10-14"), making lever operation practical
- Simple installation (4 screws, same as a water filter bracket)
- No moving parts, no service loops, no cable chains
- Tube routing is straightforward (horizontal run to main body)
- The insertion axis (left-right) is natural for side-wall mounting
- Works with the current horizontal slide-in design without changes
- Low cost — no additional hardware beyond the dock itself

### Secondary Recommendation: Pull-Out Tray (Upgrade Path)

If user testing reveals that even the side-wall reach is uncomfortable, the **pull-out tray** is the premium upgrade:
- Eliminates all reach/visibility concerns
- Adds ~$25-45 in hardware cost
- Requires a cable chain or service loop for tube routing
- Increases installation time from 15 min to 45-60 min
- Can be designed as an optional accessory — the dock mounts to the tray the same way it would mount to a wall

### Design Implication

The dock's mounting interface should be a simple **flat back plate with 4 screw holes**. This makes it compatible with:
- Direct wall mounting (side wall or back wall)
- Tray mounting (screw to a plywood tray on drawer slides)
- Door mounting (screw to door interior)
- Any future mounting scheme

The dock should NOT have integrated mounting hardware specific to one location. A universal flat-back design with 4 mounting holes is the most flexible foundation.

### Future Consideration: Vertical Insert

If the project evolves toward a more consumer-friendly cartridge change experience, switching to **vertical insertion (ceiling mount, cartridge hangs down)** would align with the proven water filter industry pattern. This would require redesigning the mating face, release plate, and lever mechanism, but would unlock the most ergonomic replacement experience. Worth revisiting after the horizontal prototype is proven.
