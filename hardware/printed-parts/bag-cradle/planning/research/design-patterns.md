# Design Patterns: How Products Support Flexible Fluid Containers

Research into how shipped consumer products, outdoor equipment, medical devices, and commercial beverage systems support flexible bags and bladders containing liquid.

---

## 1. Osprey Hydraulics Reservoir (Hydration Pack)

**Container:** Flexible TPU bladder, 2L or 3L capacity. Rectangular when filled, roughly 38-46 cm tall by 19-23 cm wide depending on capacity. Thin pillow-shaped cross-section when full.

**Support mechanism:** A rigid plastic backer plate is laminated or bonded to the rear face of the bladder. This flat stiffener spans the full height and width of the reservoir. The backer plate serves as a spine: it prevents the bladder from folding, buckling, or collapsing as it is slid into the hydration sleeve pocket in a backpack. The sleeve itself is a simple fabric pocket sewn against the back panel of the pack, sized to match the bladder footprint.

**Kink prevention at outlet:** The drinking tube exits via a quick-disconnect fitting positioned near the top of the bladder, not the bottom. This top-exit placement means the tube routes upward and over the shoulder, avoiding sharp bends. The tube never needs to make a U-turn around the bottom of the bag. The quick-disconnect also allows the tube to be detached without removing the bladder from the sleeve.

**Complete drainage:** The bladder hangs vertically in the pack with the user's back providing compression. Gravity pulls liquid downward. The drinking tube draws from the bottom of the internal chamber. As water is consumed, the flexible front face collapses toward the rigid backer plate, squeezing remaining liquid downward.

**Contoured vs. generic:** The backer plate is contoured to the specific bladder model. It matches the bladder's width and height exactly. Third-party bladders that lack this backer plate are noticeably floppier in the same sleeve.

**Install/remove:** The bladder slides into the sleeve from the top, backer plate facing the user's back. A hook or hang loop at the top clips to a webbing tab inside the pack. Removal is the reverse: unhook, slide out.

**Notable tradeoff:** The rigid backer adds weight (~1.5 oz over the backer-less Hydraulics LT model) and prevents the bladder from being rolled or folded compactly. Packed minimum size is roughly 6.5" x 7" x 1.5".

## 2. CamelBak Crux Reservoir (Hydration Pack)

**Container:** Flexible TPU bladder, available in 1.5L, 2L, and 3L. The 2L measures 38 x 19 x 6 cm; the 3L measures 46 x 23 x 8 cm.

**Support mechanism:** Instead of a full rigid backer plate, the CamelBak Crux uses an internal center baffle -- a welded seam running vertically down the middle of the bladder interior. This baffle bonds the front and back sheets together along a line, creating two side-by-side chambers that communicate at top and bottom. The effect is to flatten the bladder's profile (reducing front-to-back thickness) and distribute the water weight into two narrower lobes rather than one bulging pillow. This gives the bladder some structural rigidity without requiring an external stiffener.

**Kink prevention at outlet:** The drinking tube exits from the top-center of the bladder. A larger diameter tube (for 20% more flow per sip) is used, but reviewers note this larger tube is more prone to kinking where it exits the pack's hydration port. The tube collapses more easily than the older, narrower tube design. This is a documented weakness: the hose kinks at the point it emerges from the pack, sometimes making it seem like the reservoir is empty.

**Complete drainage:** Like the Osprey, the bladder hangs vertically and gravity feeds liquid to the bottom where the tube draws. The center baffle also reduces sloshing, which helps liquid settle to the lowest point rather than sloshing laterally.

**Contoured vs. generic:** The center baffle is integral to the bladder -- it is a feature of the container itself rather than the support structure. The backpack sleeve is generic (a simple fabric pocket).

**Install/remove:** The bladder slides into the hydration sleeve from the top. A hang tab clips to the pack's internal hook. The wide-mouth opening at the top is used for filling.

**Key lesson:** An internal baffle that flattens the bag profile can substitute for an external rigid backer, but it does not prevent the bag from folding or buckling during insertion. It is a shape-management feature, not a structural support feature.

## 3. Boxxle Box Wine Dispenser (Consumer Beverage)

**Container:** Standard 3-liter bag-in-box wine bladder. The bag is a flexible metalized film or plastic pouch with a pre-attached spigot/tap. When full, the bag is roughly the shape of a flattened rectangular pillow.

**Support mechanism:** The Boxxle is a rigid stainless steel and injection-molded plastic housing that completely encloses the wine bag. Inside the housing is a spring-loaded plate. During loading, the user presses this plate down to the bottom of the housing where it latches into place. The wine bag is placed on top of the compressed plate. When the lid is closed, a trigger releases the latch, and the spring-loaded plate pushes upward against the bottom of the bag. This constant upward pressure compresses the bag from below, pushing wine upward toward the spigot. The spring is described as "rather strong" by reviewers.

**Kink prevention at outlet:** The spigot protrudes through a shaped cutout in the front face of the housing. The bag is positioned so the spigot aligns with this cutout during installation. Because the spring plate pushes the bag upward and the spigot is at the front, the spigot is always above the glass -- gravity and spring pressure together ensure flow. There is no flexible tubing to kink.

**Complete drainage:** The spring-loaded plate continuously compresses the bag as it empties, squeezing out remaining liquid. The mechanism is designed so that nearly every drop is dispensed without the user needing to squeeze or tilt. The spring provides constant pressure throughout the emptying cycle.

**Contoured vs. generic:** The housing is a generic rectangular chamber sized to accept standard 3L bag-in-box bladders. The spring plate is flat. It does not conform to the bag's shape -- it simply pushes upward against whatever bag is placed above it.

**Install/remove:** Open lid, push spring plate down until it clicks, place bag in chamber, thread spigot through front cutout, close lid (which releases the spring). Removal is the reverse.

**Key lesson:** Active compression via a spring-loaded plate is one approach to ensuring complete drainage. However, the strong spring can puncture or tear fragile bags. One reviewer experienced a bag of Sangria leaking after the spring compressed a small pre-existing flaw in the foil bag.

## 4. Commercial Post-Mix BIB Syrup Racks (Soda Fountain Industry)

**Container:** Bag-in-box (BIB) syrup pouches, typically 2.5 to 5 gallons (roughly 9.5-19 liters). The bag is a flexible multi-layer film pouch with a molded fitment/connector built into one corner or face. The bag sits inside a corrugated cardboard box.

**Support mechanism:** Heavy-duty steel racks with either flat or inclined shelves. Racks are modular and stackable, available in 1-wide, 2-wide, or 3-wide configurations. A single-wide inclined shelf unit from Lancer measures 15"W x 12"D x 19"H. The boxes (containing the bags) sit directly on the shelves. The cardboard box itself is the immediate structural support for the bag; the rack supports the box.

**Inclined shelf variant:** Inclined shelves tilt the box so the fitment end is at the lowest point. This uses gravity to direct remaining syrup toward the outlet connector. The tilt facilitates optimal flow and dispensing of liquids.

**Kink prevention at outlet:** The fitment on BIB bags contains a one-way valve that opens only under pressure from the mating connector. There is no flexible tube between the bag and the pump that could kink -- the pump connector mates directly to the bag fitment. The pump (typically a Flojet 5000 series) draws syrup through the connector with suction.

**Complete drainage:** The inclined shelf angle directs liquid toward the fitment. The pump provides suction to evacuate the bag. The pump has an automatic shut-off that detects when the bag is empty (via vacuum). Scholle IPN, a major BIB packaging manufacturer, claims over 99% product evacuation from their bags. Their patented spout fitment design achieves this through textured surfaces on the inner flange: concentric ridges, radial grooves, and a waffle-textured pattern that create capillary flow channels. These channels prevent the flexible film from being sucked flat against the spout opening (which would seal it and stop drainage). The grooves maintain open pathways for liquid to reach the spout even as the bag collapses around it.

**Contoured vs. generic:** Entirely generic. The rack shelf is a flat (or tilted flat) steel surface. The cardboard box is a generic rectangle. The bag is loose inside the box. No contouring whatsoever.

**Install/remove:** Place the box on the shelf, connect the pump connector to the bag fitment (through a hole in the box). To replace, disconnect the connector, remove the empty box, place a new one.

**Key lesson:** The combination of (a) an inclined surface to orient the bag so the outlet is at the lowest point, (b) active suction via a pump, and (c) textured anti-collapse features on the spout fitment achieves near-complete evacuation without any contouring or cradling of the bag itself.

## 5. GOJO 800 Series Bag-in-Box Soap Dispenser (Commercial/Institutional)

**Container:** 800 mL flexible film pouch containing liquid soap. The bag has a polypropylene collar at the bottom with an integrated pump mechanism. The bag is a sealed "sanitary sealed" refill -- no cutting or piercing required.

**Support mechanism:** A wall-mounted plastic housing. The housing is a shallow rectangular box with an open front. The bag hangs vertically inside the housing, suspended by its collar which snaps into a retention slot at the bottom of the dispenser. The collar acts as both the structural mounting point and the dispensing interface. The bag hangs from the collar under gravity, with the bulk of the liquid weight above the mounting point.

**Kink prevention at outlet:** There is no flexible tubing. The collar integrates a pump mechanism (push-style) that dispenses directly. The dispensing path is entirely within the rigid collar assembly.

**Complete drainage:** Gravity. The bag hangs vertically with the outlet at the very bottom. Liquid naturally pools at the collar/pump. The user pushes a lever that actuates the pump, drawing soap from the lowest point. As the bag empties, it collapses from the top down. The hanging orientation means there are no horizontal surfaces where liquid could pool away from the outlet.

**Contoured vs. generic:** The housing is a simple rectangular cavity. It does not conform to the bag shape. The bag hangs freely. The only precise interface is the collar snap-fit.

**Install/remove:** Open the dispenser front cover, slide the bag's collar into the retention slot until it snaps into place, close the cover. To replace, open the cover, pull the collar out of the slot, discard the empty bag, snap in a new one.

**Key lesson:** Hanging a bag vertically with the outlet at the absolute bottom is the simplest and most reliable way to achieve complete gravity drainage. The bag's own weight keeps it taut. No cradle or contouring is needed. The structural interface is minimal: a single snap-fit collar.

## 6. CADD Ambulatory Infusion Pump (Medical Device)

**Container:** Rigid or semi-rigid medication cassette reservoir, 50 mL to 250 mL. The cassette is a purpose-built container with a flat rectangular form factor designed to mate directly with the pump body.

**Support mechanism:** The cassette attaches to the pump body via a hinged mechanical interface. Two hooks on the cassette insert into mating hinge pins on the pump. A cassette latch on the side of the pump rotates to lock the cassette in place. The latching mechanism provides positive retention -- it clicks firmly into the locked position, and the pump will alarm and stop delivery if the cassette becomes unlatched.

**Kink prevention at outlet:** The cassette-to-pump interface is a direct mechanical coupling. There is no flexible tubing between the cassette and the pump mechanism. Tubing runs from the pump output to the patient, not between the container and the pump.

**Complete drainage:** The pump uses a peristaltic or piston mechanism to actively draw medication from the cassette. The rigid cassette walls do not collapse, so evacuation depends entirely on the pump. The cassette shape is optimized so that the pump inlet draws from the lowest point of the internal chamber.

**Contoured vs. generic:** Highly contoured. The cassette is purpose-designed to mate with a specific pump model. The hinge pins, latch, and form factor are all model-specific. Third-party cassettes must replicate the exact mechanical interface.

**Install/remove:** Insert cassette hooks into pump hinge pins, rotate cassette until it aligns flush with the pump body, push cassette latch up until it clicks. A pump key locks the latch. Removal requires the key, then reversing the process.

**Key lesson:** When the container is purpose-designed for the support structure, the interface can be extremely precise and secure. The rigid cassette eliminates all the problems of flexible bags (kinking, pooling, shape uncertainty) but at the cost of being single-use and model-specific.

## 7. Kangaroo Joey Enteral Feeding Pump (Medical Device)

**Container:** 1000 mL flexible PVC feeding bag, roughly rectangular when filled. A pre-installed hanger hook is molded into the top of the bag.

**Support mechanism:** The bag hangs from an IV-pole-style hook or from a bedside caddy. The Kangaroo Joey pump itself does not cradle the bag -- the bag hangs above the pump, and tubing runs from the bag down to the pump. Third-party caddies (e.g., Etsy aftermarket products) provide a magnetic-base cradle that holds the pump body, with a hook arm above for hanging the bag. The bag must hang at least 6 inches above the pump for accurate feeding rates.

**Kink prevention at outlet:** The outlet tubing exits from the bottom of the bag, running straight down to the pump. The vertical orientation means the tube never needs to bend sharply. The pump's roller mechanism controls flow, so there is no reliance on gravity flow through potentially kinked tubing.

**Complete drainage:** The bag hangs vertically. Gravity collects liquid at the bottom where the outlet is. The pump provides active suction. The bag collapses from the top down as it empties.

**Contoured vs. generic:** The hanging system is entirely generic. Any hook that can hold the bag's hanger tab works. The caddy cradles the pump, not the bag.

**Install/remove:** Hang the bag on the hook, connect the tubing to the pump. To replace, disconnect tubing, remove bag from hook, hang new bag.

**Key lesson:** Separating the bag support (a simple hook) from the pump (a separate device below) gives maximum flexibility. The bag's shape does not matter to the support structure. The bag can be any size or shape, as long as it has a hook and an outlet tube.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### Theme 1: Outlet placement at the gravitational low point is the single most important factor for complete drainage

Every product that achieves good drainage places the outlet at or near the lowest point of the container during normal operation. The GOJO dispenser hangs the bag so the outlet is at the absolute bottom. The BIB racks tilt the box so the fitment is at the lowest corner. The hydration packs hang the bladder vertically so the tube draws from the bottom. When the outlet is not at the lowest point, liquid becomes trapped.

### Theme 2: Rigid backers and baffles solve the "insertion problem," not the "drainage problem"

The Osprey backer plate and CamelBak center baffle exist primarily to make the bladder easy to slide into a sleeve and to maintain a predictable form factor while packed among other gear. They contribute minimally to drainage. Drainage is driven by gravity and the user sucking through the tube. The structural features serve handling and packing, not fluid mechanics.

### Theme 3: Active compression or suction compensates for imperfect geometry

When the outlet cannot be at the absolute lowest point, or when the bag cannot hang freely, active mechanisms compensate. The Boxxle uses a spring-loaded plate to squeeze wine toward the spigot. The BIB soda fountain uses a suction pump. The CADD infusion pump uses a piston mechanism. In all these cases, the mechanism provides positive pressure or vacuum that overcomes gravity traps. Passive (gravity-only) systems demand more precise geometry.

### Theme 4: Anti-collapse features at the outlet are critical for flexible bags

The Scholle IPN patent reveals that when a flexible bag collapses, the film can seal over the outlet, stopping flow entirely even when liquid remains. Their solution -- concentric ridges, radial grooves, and waffle textures on the spout flange -- creates permanent flow channels that the collapsing film cannot block. This is a problem unique to flexible containers that rigid containers never face. Any cradle design for flexible bags must consider what happens at the outlet as the bag reaches its last 10-20% of capacity.

### Theme 5: Generic supports work; contoured supports add handling convenience

The BIB rack is a flat steel shelf. The GOJO housing is a simple rectangular box. The Kangaroo Joey hook is a simple wire. None of these conform to the bag shape, and all work well. The Osprey backer plate is contoured, but its contouring serves ease-of-insertion rather than fluid management. The CADD cassette is highly contoured, but only because it must mechanically mate with the pump. For gravity-drained flexible bags, generic supports are sufficient as long as the bag can find its natural shape.

### Theme 6: The bag-support interface should be minimal and mechanical

The most reliable installations have a single, positive mechanical interface between the bag and the support. The GOJO collar snaps into a slot. The CADD cassette hooks into hinge pins. The Kangaroo bag hangs from a hook tab. The BIB box sits on a shelf. In every case, the installation is tool-free, intuitive, and unambiguous. Complex multi-point retention systems are absent from successful products.

### Theme 7: Tubing should exit the bag at the point closest to the support structure

In hydration packs, the tube exits near the top where the hang hook is, preventing the tube from being pulled by the bag's weight. In the GOJO dispenser, the pump is integral to the collar that mates with the housing. In BIB systems, the connector goes through the box wall right at the shelf surface. When tubing has to traverse a long unsupported span between the bag and the fixed plumbing, it becomes vulnerable to kinking and pulling.

### Theme 8: The failure mode of active compression is bag damage

The Boxxle's strong spring was documented to rupture a wine bag that had a minor pre-existing flaw in the foil. Active compression amplifies any weakness in the bag material. Passive systems (gravity, gentle cradling) are gentler on the container. This is relevant for thin-walled bags like the Platypus collapsible bottle.

### Theme 9: Vertical hanging with bottom outlet is the dominant pattern for small bags (under 5L)

The GOJO, Kangaroo Joey, and IV bag systems all use this pattern. It is simple, self-draining, and requires minimal support structure. The bag's own weight keeps it taut and oriented correctly. The support is just a hook or clip at the top. This pattern breaks down for larger or heavier bags where the hanging load becomes problematic, or where vertical space is limited.

### Theme 10: Inclined shelf with low-point outlet is the dominant pattern for larger bags in enclosed spaces

The BIB soda fountain rack uses an inclined shelf. The Boxxle uses an internal platform (effectively a horizontal shelf with upward compression). When vertical hanging is not possible due to space constraints, an angled shelf that directs liquid toward the outlet is the standard approach. The angle does not need to be steep -- even a modest tilt ensures liquid migrates to the low corner over time.
