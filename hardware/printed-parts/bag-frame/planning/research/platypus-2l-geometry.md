### Thickness calculation

The bag is two flat sheets welded at the perimeter. At 2L capacity, with a face area of roughly 190 mm x 300 mm (effective fill zone, excluding the narrow taper to spout and the sealed bottom margin), a perfectly rectangular fill would give:

    thickness = volume / (width x length) = 2,000,000 mm^3 / (190 mm x 300 mm) = ~35 mm

In reality the cross-section is a lens/lenticular shape (not rectangular), so the peak thickness at the widest midsection is greater than 35 mm to compensate for the curved edges. Estimated peak midsection thickness when filled to 2L: **38-42 mm**. The gusseted bottom adds ~5 mm of additional width at the base.

### Film behavior

- **Stiffness**: The film is "somewhat stiff" when holding liquid, providing enough rigidity to stand upright when filled. However it is fully flexible -- it can be folded, rolled, and creased without damage.
- **Durability**: Manufacturer claims it withstands freezing and boiling. Reviewers report surviving 12-foot drops onto concrete and requiring multiple baseball bat strikes to rupture. Lifetime warranty.
- **Crease behavior**: Fold creases may develop with extended use, particularly near the spout area. These are cosmetic and do not typically cause leaks but represent potential long-term fatigue points.
- **Transparency**: Clear/translucent with printed branding. Volume markings visible on the side.


## 4. Seam Patterns

### Perimeter seam

A continuous welded seam runs around the entire perimeter of the bag where the two film panels are joined. This is a heat-weld (not adhesive). The weld width is estimated at **3-5 mm** based on product photos. The seam is the structural boundary of the bag -- liquid fills up to but not into the seam area.

### Bottom seal (opposite spout)

The bottom end is a **flat heat-seal** running the full width of the bag. It includes a **gusset** -- an inward fold of film that allows the base to expand into a more rectangular cross-section when filled, enabling the bag to stand upright. The gusset creates a small triangular "ear" of excess material at each bottom corner. Some users punch holes in these ears for hanging cord.

The bottom seal is:
- A straight horizontal weld across the full 190 mm width
- Located approximately 5-8 mm from the absolute bottom edge of the film
- The gusset fold adds approximately 15-20 mm of depth to the base when expanded

### Side seams

The side seams run vertically from the bottom seal to the spout area. Near the top, the bag tapers inward from the full 190 mm width to the ~30-35 mm spout collar over approximately 40-60 mm of height. This taper creates a "shoulder" shape with angled seams converging on the spout.


## 5. Cross-Section Shape When Filled

### At widest midsection (~50% height)

**Lenticular / lens-shaped cross-section.** Two convex arcs meeting at the welded seam edges. The peak thickness at center is ~38-42 mm, tapering to zero at the side seams. This is the natural shape of two flat sheets pressurized from within -- they bulge into symmetric convex curves.

### Near the spout (top 15%)

The width narrows from 190 mm to the ~30-35 mm spout collar. The cross-section becomes more circular/tubular as it approaches the spout, with thickness roughly equal to width in the final 20 mm before the spout.

### Near the sealed end (bottom 15%)

The gusset allows the bottom to expand into a more **rectangular / stadium-shaped** cross-section. The gusset fold creates a flat bottom surface approximately 50-70 mm wide, with the sides curving up. This is what allows the bag to stand upright. Thickness at the gusseted base: ~25-30 mm.

### Key insight for frame design

The cross-section is NOT uniform along the length. It transitions from:
- Tubular (near spout) -> lenticular (midsection) -> stadium/rectangular (gusseted base)


## 6. Behavior at 35 Degrees from Horizontal (Cap End Down)

### Liquid pooling

At 35 degrees with the cap end (spout) pointing down, liquid pools heavily toward the cap end. The bottom half of the bag (spout side) becomes taut and fully distended while the upper portion (sealed end) partially collapses as liquid drains away from it.

### Deformation characteristics

- The bag does not maintain its "standing upright" shape when tilted -- the gusseted base collapses since gravity is no longer aligned with it
- The midsection cross-section shifts from centered-lenticular to an asymmetric shape, with the downhill side bulging more than the uphill side
- The sealed (top) end, if only partially filled, will fold or crease as air occupies the upper portion
- The bag becomes a roughly **wedge-shaped pillow**: thick and taut at the low (spout) end, progressively thinner toward the high (sealed) end
- Without external constraint, the filled portion at the low end could bulge to **40-45 mm thickness** since all 2L of liquid is concentrated in a shorter effective length
- With the vision.md constraint frame (25-30 mm target), the frame must spread the liquid load more uniformly by preventing localized bulging

### Where liquid collects

At 35 degrees, roughly 70-80% of the liquid volume will be in the lower half of the bag (spout side). The upper 20-30% of the bag length may be nearly empty and folded flat.

### Implications for frame design

The frame must:
1. Prevent the lower (spout) end from bulging beyond ~30 mm thickness
2. Support the bag film at the upper (sealed) end to maintain predictable shape even when nearly empty
3. Account for the fact that the bag's "natural" shape at 35 degrees is very different from its upright shape

| Fold behavior | Rolls and folds predictably | Reviewer consensus |
| Crumple behavior | Does not crumple randomly; the film has enough stiffness to hold folds | Reviewer observation |

### Folding pattern

When empty, the bag folds predictably along its width. Most users roll from the sealed end toward the spout, creating a compact cylinder. The film memory is minimal -- it does not spring back aggressively. Repeated folding in the same creases can eventually create permanent fold lines.

### Relevance to frame design

When a bag needs replacement (refill scenario), the collapsed bag slides out easily. The frame does not need to accommodate a rigid shape for insertion/removal -- the bag is fully limp when empty.

### Load considerations at 35 degrees

At 35 degrees, the gravitational component along the incline is sin(35) x weight = ~0.574 x 2.0 kg = ~1.15 kg pulling toward the spout end. The normal force pressing the bag into the frame is cos(35) x weight = ~0.819 x 2.0 kg = ~1.64 kg. These are modest loads that any reasonable 3D-printed frame can handle.
