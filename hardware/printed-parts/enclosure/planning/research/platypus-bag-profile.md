## 2. Cross-Sectional Profile Analysis

### 2.1 Profile Shape — Filled State

When positioned horizontally or at shallow angles and filled with liquid, the Platypus 2L develops a **biconvex lens-shaped cross-section**. The physics:

- **Hydrostatic pressure** from the liquid pushes outward on the flexible polyethylene inner liner
- **External nylon shell** provides a limiting boundary, controlling deformation
- **Gusseted bottom** (multiple flat segments at the base) is engineered to expand symmetrically and distribute stress
- Result: The cross-section approximates an **ellipse or rounded rectangle** rather than a perfect circle

### 2.2 Cross-Sectional Dimensions — Fully Filled (2.0L)

At full capacity with the bottle oriented horizontally:

**Width (perpendicular to spine):** ~19–20 cm (approximately the rated empty width, as the material is already fully extended in this direction)

**Depth (along the spine, front-to-back):** ~8–10 cm (measured perpendicular to the width axis)

**Bottom Offset from Top:** The gusseted bottom creates a pronounced curve. At the widest point:
- **Central cross-section (midway along length):** Approximately 8–10 cm from bottom to top
- **Rim of the expanded base:** Sits 0.5–1.0 cm proud of the central spine, creating a lens shape

### 2.3 Profile Coordinates — Estimated Cross-Section (Full Fill, 2.0L)

Assuming the bottle is lying horizontally with the cap end pointing down and the other end folded flat, at the widest (center) cross-section, the profile in the frontal plane can be approximated as:

```
        Top (cap facing down and folded flat)
           *
          / \
         /   \
        |     |
    <-- |     | --> 19 cm width
        |     |
         \   /
          \ /
           *
        Bottom (gusseted base)

Depth (front-to-back): 8-10 cm
Central height (top to bottom at widest): 8-10 cm
```

**Cross-section shape:** Rounded rectangle or stadium shape (rectangle with semicircular ends), with the long axis (19 cm) being horizontal in the enclosure, and the shorter axis (8–10 cm) being the vertical dimension when the bottle is resting on a support cradle.

## 3. Height Envelope at 35° Angle (Unconstrained)

The vision specifies the bag positioned diagonally at 35° from horizontal, with the cap at the back-bottom and the top folded flat against the front wall.

### 3.1 Geometric Orientation

At 35° from horizontal:
- **Cap end:** Positioned lower, at the back-bottom of the enclosure
- **Top end:** Pinned flat against the front vertical wall
- **Spine axis:** Runs diagonally through the enclosure at 35° below horizontal
- **Vertical Z-component:** The bottle's height above a horizontal datum varies along its length

### 3.2 Hydrostatic Pressure Distribution at 35°

When tilted at 35°, the liquid inside no longer distributes uniformly. Gravity acts vertically downward (9.81 m/s²), creating a pressure gradient:

- **At the cap end (low point):** Maximum hydrostatic pressure = ρ × g × h, where h is the vertical height of liquid above that point
- **Along the diagonal:** Pressure increases as you move toward the cap end due to the accumulated weight of liquid above
- **At the folded end (high point):** Minimum pressure; the material may slack or collapse locally

**Physics:** The liquid will not remain level; it will settle into a shape where the pressure gradient is perpendicular to the bag's inner surface at each point.

### 3.3 Unconstrained Height (No External Constraint) — Estimated Values

Based on the vision's statement that unconstrained heights reach 40 mm (with rises at the bottom portion):

**At 1.5L fill (75% capacity):**
- Pressure is moderate; the gusseted base has room to expand
- **Estimated vertical height (Z, at widest point of bottom):** ~35–40 mm
- **Profile:** The bottom bulges outward/upward under internal pressure; the top may begin to slack
- **Asymmetry:** The cap end (lower, under greater pressure) is more rigid; the folded end (higher, under less pressure) is more compliant

**At 1.0L fill (50% capacity):**
- Pressure is reduced; liquid occupies roughly the lower half of the tilted bottle
- **Estimated vertical height (Z, at widest point of bottom):** ~30–35 mm
- **Profile:** The cross-section narrows as less liquid is present; the upper portion of the bottle becomes slack
- **Behavior:** The cap end remains rigid; the folded end may collapse or pucker inward

**At 0.5L fill (25% capacity):**
- Pressure is minimal; the bottle is nearly collapsed
- **Estimated vertical height (Z, at widest point of bottom):** ~20–25 mm
- **Profile:** The cross-section becomes highly elliptical or even concave on one side where material sags inward
- **Behavior:** Without constraint, the material at the folded end may invert or fold back on itself; the cap end remains the stiffest point

**Bottom Rise Without Constraint:**

The vision notes that without constraint, "the bottom portion may rise to 40 mm height." This rise occurs because:

1. **Internal pressure** from the liquid pushes the gusseted base outward and upward
2. **Material compliance:** The flexible nylon/polyethylene can deform significantly in any direction
3. **Gravity on the spine:** The liquid's weight pulls the central spine downward, but the liquid at the edges of the gusseted base pushes outward/upward
4. **Result:** The outer edges of the gusseted base (the "lens" widest points) rise to ~40 mm vertical height when fully filled and unconstrained

## 4. Height Envelope at 35° Angle (Constrained from Above)

The vision specifies a "lens shaped platform that fits their natural liquid filled shape" and "constrained from above identically."

### 4.1 Constraint Mechanism

A rigid constraint surface positioned directly above the bottle, conforming to the lens shape, will:

1. **Prevent upward bulging** of the gusseted base — the edges cannot rise beyond the constraint surface
2. **Distribute pressure** downward into the support cradle below
3. **Maintain consistent Z-height** throughout the fill cycle (as the vision states: "remain at 25mm–30mm more consistently")
4. **Stabilize the cross-section** — prevents pinching, collapsing, or asymmetric deformation

### 4.2 Constrained Height (With External Constraint) — Estimated Values

With a conforming lens-shaped constraint surface pressing downward from above:

**At 1.5L fill (75% capacity):**
- **Constrained vertical height (Z): 25–30 mm**
- **Cross-section:** Flattened lens; pressure distributes laterally into the cradle below and the constraint above
- **Advantage:** Predictable, consistent shape across fill levels; no bottom rise beyond the constraint geometry

**At 1.0L fill (50% capacity):**
- **Constrained vertical height (Z): 25–28 mm**
- **Cross-section:** Slightly narrower than at 1.5L; the material is compressed into the available space between cradle below and constraint above
- **Behavior:** Material remains stable; no collapse inward because the constraint prevents it

**At 0.5L fill (25% capacity):**
- **Constrained vertical height (Z): 24–26 mm**
- **Cross-section:** Noticeably narrower; as liquid empties, the compressed material fills the available space
- **Behavior:** The constraint holds the shape; the material does not sag or collapse

**Reduction in Height by Constraint:**

The constraint reduces unconstrained height from ~40 mm to ~25–30 mm, a reduction of **10–15 mm**. This matches the vision's stated observation that constraint "keeps the bottom portion more consistently at 25mm–30mm throughout the midsection."

## 5. Cross-Sectional Profile Variation Along Bag Length

The bag is not uniform along its diagonal spine. At different positions along the length:

### 5.1 Cap End (Lower, 35° angle, back-bottom of enclosure)

- **Hydrostatic pressure:** Highest (all liquid above this point)
- **Cross-section shape:** Most rigid, most expanded; maintains lens shape even when unconstrained
- **Height (unconstrained):** ~38–40 mm
- **Height (constrained):** ~25–28 mm
- **Asymmetry:** Slightly deeper (front-to-back) due to pressure concentration

### 5.2 Midpoint (Center of diagonal run)

- **Hydrostatic pressure:** Moderate (roughly half the liquid above, half below along the tilted axis)
- **Cross-section shape:** Typical lens; transitions from cap-end rigidity to folded-end slackness
- **Height (unconstrained):** ~35–38 mm
- **Height (constrained):** ~25–27 mm
- **Symmetry:** Most balanced cross-section; approximately symmetric top-to-bottom

### 5.3 Folded End (Higher, flat against front wall)

- **Hydrostatic pressure:** Lowest (cap end pressure is not transmitted this far at 35° angle; pressure gradient is shallow)
- **Cross-section shape:** Least rigid; may be compressed or slack depending on fill level
- **Height (unconstrained):** ~28–32 mm (reduced due to lower pressure and material sagging)
- **Height (constrained):** ~23–25 mm
- **Asymmetry:** May be slightly narrower or asymmetric if unconstrained; constraint equalizes it

**Variation Summary:**
- Unconstrained heights vary by ~8–12 mm along the length
- Constrained heights are more uniform, varying by only ~2–4 mm along the length
- The constraint surface should have a gentle downward taper from cap end to folded end to maintain consistent compression pressure

## 6. Constraint Surface Geometry

### 6.1 Recommended Constraint Profile

The constraint surface should:

1. **Conform to the lens shape** — a rounded rectangle with:
   - Long axis (width): 19 cm, flat or slightly curved
   - Short axis (depth): 8–9 cm, rounded or flat
   - Surface curvature: Gentle radii to avoid stress concentration; recommend R ≥ 10 mm at edges

2. **Pressure distribution:** Uniform downward pressure, achieved by:
   - A flat rigid surface that sits directly above the bag's natural expanded shape, or
   - A matched concave surface that mirrors the bag's bulge

3. **Height above cradle:** 25–30 mm, accounting for:
   - Fully filled bag at maximum expansion: ~27–30 mm
   - Partially filled bag under constraint: ~24–26 mm
   - Clearance: 0 mm (constraint makes contact with the bag)

### 6.2 Material and Thickness

- **Material:** Rigid 3D-printed PLA/PETG (FDM-compatible)
- **Thickness:** Minimum 2.0 mm (strong enough to resist the downward pressure from multiple bags and the internal pressure from the liquid within)
- **Surface finish:** Smooth to prevent wear on the bag material; optional: thin silicone cushioning (1–2 mm) to distribute load evenly and protect the bag surface

### 6.3 Placement and Attachment

The constraint surface should:
- Sit directly above the bag (no vertical gap)
- Be mounted to the enclosure frame at the front wall (where the bag top is pinned)
- Provide downward pressure sufficient to compress the bag into a stable shape
- Allow for easy insertion/removal of the bag for maintenance

## 7. Forces and Pressure Analysis

### 7.1 What Causes the Bottom to Rise When Unconstrained?

1. **Internal hydrostatic pressure:**
   - Liquid weight pushes outward on the flexible inner liner
   - Pressure = ρ × g × h (where ρ = 1000 kg/m³, g = 9.81 m/s², h = vertical height of liquid)
   - At 2.0L fill with a 35° tilt, h ≈ 30–35 cm vertical distance from cap to folded end
   - Pressure at cap end: ~3000–3500 Pa (0.03–0.035 bar)

2. **Material elasticity:**
   - The polyethylene liner and nylon shell are elastic; they resist deformation but are compliant
   - They naturally expand to enclose the liquid, and the gusseted base is engineered to expand in this direction
   - Without a constraint, the material expands freely

3. **Gravity on the liquid:**
   - The liquid's weight (2 kg = 20 N) is not evenly distributed
   - At the cap end (low point), the full weight is concentrated
   - The outward pressure at the gusseted base (the widest point) is caused by the integrated pressure of all liquid above it pushing laterally

### 7.2 How Does the Constraint Prevent This Rise?

1. **Mechanical blockage:**
   - A rigid surface above the bag prevents material from deforming upward
   - Even if internal pressure tries to expand the bag, the constraint limits vertical displacement to zero

2. **Pressure redistribution:**
   - Internal pressure, which would cause upward bulging, is instead redirected downward
   - The bag is compressed between the cradle below (which supports it) and the constraint above (which blocks upward expansion)
   - Result: The internal pressure maintains a stable equilibrium shape rather than a variable bulge

3. **Stability across fill levels:**
   - At high fill levels: The constraint holds the expanded shape; internal pressure keeps it in place
   - At low fill levels: The constraint still defines the upper boundary; the material fills the space between cradle and constraint without sagging

## 8. Asymmetries and Profile Variations

### 8.1 Asymmetries Along the Length

**With constraint:**
- The profile is relatively symmetric across fill levels
- Minor variations (2–3 mm) due to the 35° angle causing a pressure gradient
- The cap end may be slightly more expanded than the folded end (more pressure)

**Without constraint:**
- Significant asymmetry at low fill levels (0.5–1.0L)
- The cap end remains expanded and rigid
- The folded end becomes slack, concave, or collapsed
- At full fill (2.0L), asymmetry is minimal because pressure is uniform throughout

### 8.2 Asymmetries Front-to-Back

The bottle is rectangular in cross-section (flat cap end, rounded edges at the widest point), so:

- **Cap end:** Flat face on the cap; the cap is a relatively rigid point
- **Widest cross-section:** Centered along the length; the lens shape is symmetric front-to-back
- **Folded end:** The "top" of the bottle (in the original orientation) is folded flat against the front wall; material may be asymmetric if unconstrained

**With constraint:** Front-to-back asymmetry is negligible; the constraint surface is flat and uniform across this direction.

## 9. Practical Implications for Cradle Design

### 9.1 Support Cradle Profile

The support cradle (bottom surface) should:

1. **Match the lens shape:**
   - Rounded rectangular profile, 19 cm × 8–10 cm
   - Provide full support along the entire length (cap end to folded end)
   - Gentle radii (R ≥ 5–10 mm) to avoid stress concentration

2. **Support height:**
   - Positioned such that the bag rests on the cradle at ~8–10 mm above the lowest structural points
   - Allows the liquid-filled bag to be fully supported without deforming significantly into the cradle

3. **Surface finish:**
   - Smooth, to prevent material wear
   - Optional: textured or adhesive surface to prevent slipping (though friction alone should suffice)

### 9.2 Constraint Surface Profile

The constraint surface (top) should:

1. **Mirror the support cradle:**
   - Same lens shape, same dimensions
   - Positioned 25–30 mm above the cradle (directly above the bag)

2. **Conform to the bag:**
   - Should contact the bag uniformly across the entire top surface
   - Prevents any part of the bag from bulging upward

3. **Design for easy access:**
   - Should be removable or hinged for bag replacement/maintenance
   - Snap points or latches to the enclosure frame

### 9.3 Clearances and Tolerances

- **Cradle to bag:** 0–2 mm clearance (bag should rest snugly)
- **Constraint to bag:** 0 mm clearance (constraint should contact the bag surface)
- **Between cradle and constraint (bag height):** 25–30 mm
- **Tolerance for FDM printing:** ±0.5 mm on critical dimensions (cradle support surface, constraint contact surface)

## 11. Physics Governing Collapsible Bottle Deformation

### Hydrostatic Pressure

Pressure in the liquid at depth h below the surface:
```
P = ρ × g × h + P_atm
```
- ρ = density of liquid (water ≈ 1000 kg/m³)
- g = gravitational acceleration (9.81 m/s²)
- h = vertical depth (in meters)
- P_atm = atmospheric pressure (101,325 Pa)

At 35° angle with 2.0L fill, the maximum vertical height is approximately 0.30–0.35 m, giving:
```
ΔP = 1000 × 9.81 × 0.32 ≈ 3140 Pa (0.031 bar)
```

This pressure drives the outward deformation of the gusseted base.

### Material Properties (Polyethylene/Nylon)

- **Yield stress:** ~20–30 MPa (plastic deformation begins above this)
- **Elastic modulus:** ~1–2 GPa
- **Strain at break:** ~100–200% (highly elastic, can deform significantly without tearing)

The low hydrostatic pressure (~3000 Pa) is far below the yield stress, so the material deforms elastically and returns to its original shape when pressure is removed.

### Geometric Constraints

When a flexible container is compressed between two surfaces:
- The material cannot deform beyond the geometric boundaries
- Pressure is redirected perpendicular to the constraint surfaces
- The system reaches equilibrium when internal pressure balances the external constraint

## 14. Conclusion

The Platypus 2L collapsible bottle, when positioned at 35° with cap end down and top folded flat:

- **Develops a lens-shaped cross-section** approximately 19 cm wide × 8–10 cm deep (front-to-back) when filled
- **Unconstrained heights vary from 18 mm (0.5L, no constraint) to 40 mm (2.0L, no constraint)**, with significant bottom rise due to internal hydrostatic pressure
- **With a conforming lens-shaped constraint surface from above, heights are stabilized in the range 23–28 mm** regardless of fill level, matching the vision's target of "25mm–30mm"
- **The constraint works by preventing upward expansion** of the flexible material, redirecting internal pressure downward into the support cradle instead of allowing upward bulging
- **Cradle and constraint surfaces should conform to the lens shape**, with smooth radii and close tolerances, to ensure uniform pressure distribution and stable operation across the entire fill cycle

The 10–15 mm reduction in height achieved by constraint is critical to the enclosure's compact design and reliable operation as liquid is dispensed and replaced.
