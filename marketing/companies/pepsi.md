# PepsiCo Competitive Analysis (Canned Soda as Indirect Competitor)

*Compiled April 6, 2026. Sources include PepsiCo 10-K/10-Q SEC filings, quarterly earnings releases, SodaStream.com product listings, Beverage Marketing Corp data, Aluminum Association recycling reports, and market research from Statista, Grand View Research, and Fact.MR.*

---

## Executive Summary

PepsiCo is the second-largest carbonated soft drink company in the world and the largest food and beverage company overall ($93.9B revenue, FY2025). Their canned soda is the product our target customer currently buys instead of using our machine. PepsiCo is simultaneously our indirect competitor (cans) and our unwitting supplier (SodaStream concentrate). They acquired SodaStream for $3.2B in 2018, wrote down ~$862M of that value by 2022, and continue to flag further impairment risk. They sell brand-name Pepsi, Mountain Dew, and Diet Mountain Dew formulations as SodaStream concentrate to home consumers -- the exact product our machine dispenses. Despite owning SodaStream, having the syrup, and having unlimited resources, PepsiCo has shown no signal of building a plumbed-in home soda dispensing system. Their incentive structure actively discourages it: every home dispense cannibalizes a high-margin can sale.

## PepsiCo Beverages North America (PBNA)

PBNA is PepsiCo's largest segment by revenue. It encompasses all beverage sales in the United States and Canada -- carbonated soft drinks, Gatorade, Aquafina, Bubly, juice, tea, coffee, and energy drinks sold across all packaging formats (cans, bottles, fountain, vending).

### Segment Financials

| Metric | FY2023 | FY2024 | FY2025 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Net revenue | $27.6B | ~$27.8B | $28.2B |
| Operating profit | $2,584M | $2,302M | $1,089M |
| Operating margin | 9.35% | 8.29% | 3.86% |

The margin collapse in FY2025 is striking. PBNA accounted for only 8% of PepsiCo's total operating profit despite generating 30% of total revenue. The segment is under severe pressure from increased promotional spending, commodity costs, and flat-to-declining volumes.

### Carbonated Soft Drink Revenue (Estimated)

PepsiCo does not break out CSD-only revenue. However, carbonated soft drinks (Pepsi, Mountain Dew, Starry, Mug) represent roughly 50-55% of the PBNA portfolio by revenue, with the remainder split across Gatorade, water, juice, tea, and other non-carb beverages.

**Estimated PepsiCo CSD revenue, North America: $14-16 billion/year.**

Of that, cans and bottles (single-serve and multi-pack retail) account for roughly 65-70% of CSD revenue, with fountain/foodservice making up the rest.

**Estimated PepsiCo canned/bottled retail CSD revenue, North America: $9-11 billion/year.**

## Volume Trends

### Overall CSD Volume

- U.S. CSD volume declined 0.2% in 2024, continuing a two-decade pattern of flat-to-declining volumes.
- Dollar sales grew 4.8% in the same period, driven entirely by price increases, not unit growth.
- The category has been structurally flat since approximately 2005, with volume declines offset by pricing power.

### Diet and Zero Sugar -- The Growth Story

- Zero-sugar variants are the primary growth engine within CSD portfolios.
- Coca-Cola Zero Sugar grew 14% in volume for full-year 2025.
- The "zero sugar" rebrand (away from "diet") has been effective: Millennials show 55% preference for zero-sugar variants over traditional diet formulations.
- The global sugar-free soda market is projected to grow from $5.7B (2025) to $10.2B (2032), a 10.4% CAGR.
- Diet/zero-sugar now represents approximately 30% of all CSD volume in the U.S., up from ~25% five years ago.

### PepsiCo-Specific Trends

- Pepsi Zero Sugar has been PepsiCo's most promoted CSD brand, with significant marketing investment and packaging redesigns.
- Diet Mountain Dew and Mountain Dew Zero Sugar both maintain strong niche followings.
- PepsiCo is launching prebiotic cola and "better-for-you" formulations -- hedging against the long-term decline of traditional CSD.

## The SodaStream Acquisition

### Deal Summary

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Announced | August 20, 2018 |
| Closed | December 2018 |
| Price | $3.2 billion ($144/share, all cash) |
| SodaStream revenue at time of acquisition | ~$543M (2017) |
| Price/revenue multiple | ~5.9x |
| Strategic rationale | Healthier portfolio, environmental positioning, at-home consumption trend |

### What Happened Next

PepsiCo's thesis was that SodaStream would ride three tailwinds: health consciousness (less sugar), environmental concern (fewer cans/bottles), and at-home consumption. The COVID-19 pandemic briefly validated this -- SodaStream saw a spike in 2020-2021 as people stayed home.

Then it fell apart.

### Impairment Timeline

- **2022**: PepsiCo recorded a major impairment charge of approximately $862 million against SodaStream brand intangible assets and goodwill. This created a 400-percentage-point unfavorable impact on operating profit in the Europe segment (where SodaStream is reported). The Europe segment posted a $1.38 billion operating loss for the year.
- **2023**: Additional impairment charges were recorded against SodaStream brand assets and goodwill. The prior-year comparison showed $862M in favorable impact from non-recurrence of the 2022 charge, but new (smaller) impairments were still taken.
- **2024-2025**: PepsiCo continues to flag SodaStream goodwill as at risk of further impairment. The language in 10-K filings is explicit: "there could be further impairment to the carrying value of the SodaStream reporting unit goodwill if future sales and their contributions to operating profit do not achieve expected future cash flows."

### Cumulative Write-Down

The total cumulative impairment against the $3.2B acquisition likely exceeds $1 billion. PepsiCo has written down roughly a third of the SodaStream acquisition value. The brand is not performing to plan.

### Why SodaStream Underperformed

The fundamental problem with SodaStream has not changed since Derek abandoned his 15 years ago: **warm water cannot hold carbonation**. SodaStream carbonates room-temperature water in an open bottle. The CO2 escapes within hours. This is not a product quality issue -- it is physics. No amount of marketing, brand partnerships, or Pepsi-branded syrups can fix the fact that the water goes flat.

SodaStream's core product loop -- carbonate, cap, refrigerate, consume within hours, repeat -- requires more effort than grabbing a can from the fridge and produces a worse result. The people who stick with SodaStream are enthusiasts who enjoy the ritual. The mass market tried it and went back to cans.

This is the most important section for our product.

| Pepsi Wild Cherry | No | SodaStream.com, Amazon (launched 2025) |
| Mountain Dew | No (sugar) | SodaStream.com, Amazon, Target |
| Diet Mountain Dew | Yes | SodaStream.com, Amazon, Target |
| Mountain Dew Zero Sugar | Yes | SodaStream.com, Amazon, Target |
| Mountain Dew Code Red | No | SodaStream.com, Amazon |
| Mountain Dew Code Red Zero Sugar | Yes | SodaStream.com, Amazon |
| Starry | No | SodaStream.com, Amazon |
| Mug Root Beer | No | SodaStream.com, Amazon |

### Why This Matters

- These are **real Pepsi formulations**, not off-brand approximations. Diet Mountain Dew SodaStream syrup made by Pepsi is Diet Mountain Dew.
- The concentrate is sold at a 1:20 ratio (syrup:water), in 14.9 fl oz bottles (440mL).
- No business license is required. Available to any home consumer.
- Pricing is approximately $6-8 per bottle, making roughly 9 liters of finished soda.
- This solves the bag-in-box problem: Pepsi and Coca-Cola will not sell commercial syrup to home consumers without a business license. But they sell the same formulations through SodaStream at retail.

### Supply Chain Risk

Our product depends on PepsiCo continuing to sell SodaStream concentrate at retail. This is a real risk with several mitigating factors:

**Risks:**
- PepsiCo could discontinue the SodaStream syrup line if SodaStream itself is shut down or sold.
- PepsiCo could restrict the retail channel or change the formulation.
- PepsiCo could notice our product and attempt to cut off supply (unlikely at current scale).

**Mitigations:**
- The syrup is sold through major retail channels (Amazon, Target, Walmart). Cutting off retail distribution would require a strategic decision, not a targeted action.
- PepsiCo has been expanding the SodaStream flavor lineup (Mountain Dew added 2024, Wild Cherry 2025), not contracting it. The trend is toward more flavors, not fewer.
- Our volume is negligible relative to SodaStream's total syrup sales. We would not register as a blip.
- Alternative concentrate sources exist (various SodaStream-compatible syrups from other brands), though they are not the real Pepsi formulation.

## Consumer Behavior: Why People Still Buy Cans

### What People Love About Cans

- **Zero effort**: Open fridge, grab can, pop tab, drink. No machine, no mixing, no cleanup, no CO2 refills.
- **Consistent quality**: Every can is identical. Same carbonation, same temperature (if refrigerated), same flavor. No user error possible.
- **Portability**: Cans go in a cooler, a lunch bag, a car cupholder. Home-dispensed soda does not leave the kitchen.
- **Sensory experience**: 85% of consumers say cans preserve flavor and carbonation better than bottles. The cold aluminum, the crack of the tab, the first sip -- this is a ritualized experience.
- **No upfront cost**: A 12-pack costs $6-8. A home carbonation system costs $100-500+ before the first sip.

### What People Hate About Cans

- **Hauling**: A heavy soda habit means carrying 24-48 cans per week from store to kitchen. This is physical labor.
- **Storage**: 12-packs take up significant refrigerator and pantry space.
- **Running out**: There is always a moment when you open the fridge and the shelf is empty. The store run is never convenient.
- **Waste**: 61 billion aluminum cans were thrown away (not recycled) in the U.S. in 2023. The recycling rate has dropped to 43%, the lowest in 25 years. Each can represents embedded energy from mining, smelting, and manufacturing.
- **Cost over time**: At $0.50-0.75 per can, a 2-can-per-day habit costs $365-550/year. Home dispensing is significantly cheaper per serving.
- **Guilt**: Health-conscious consumers feel conflicted about their soda habit. The can is a visible symbol of that habit -- a physical object that accumulates in recycling bins and trash.

### What Would Make Them Switch

The barrier to switching from cans to home dispensing is not taste or cost -- it is **effort and reliability**. Consumers will switch when:

1. The home option requires less effort than a store run (our product: turn the handle).
2. The result is indistinguishable from or better than the canned product (our product: same formulation, colder, fizzier).
3. The system never runs out unexpectedly (syrup lasts weeks, CO2 tank lasts months).
4. The upfront cost is justified by the per-serving savings.

## Environmental Angle

### The Can Waste Problem

- 155.8 billion aluminum cans shipped in the U.S. in 2024.
- Only 43% are recycled (2023 data). This is a 25-year low.
- 61 billion cans per year go to landfill -- $1.2 billion worth of aluminum.
- States with bottle deposit laws achieve 68% recycling rates vs. 22% in non-deposit states.
- Primary aluminum production requires strip mining bauxite and enormous energy for smelting.

### Our Environmental Positioning

A home soda machine that dispenses from concentrate eliminates the can entirely. One 14.9 oz bottle of SodaStream concentrate replaces approximately 25 twelve-ounce cans. The environmental math is overwhelming:

- A 2-can-per-day household discards ~730 cans per year.
- Our system replaces those 730 cans with approximately 29 concentrate bottles (recyclable plastic or glass) and CO2 tank refills.
- The reduction in aluminum waste, transportation energy, and packaging is roughly 95%.

This is not the primary selling point (convenience is), but it is a powerful secondary message.

## Competitive Threat Assessment: Could PepsiCo Build This?

### What PepsiCo Has

- **SodaStream**: They own the leading home carbonation brand.
- **The syrup**: They already make and sell brand-name concentrate.
- **Brand permission**: Consumers would trust a PepsiCo-branded home soda machine.
- **Capital**: Unlimited. $93.9B in revenue. They could fund any hardware project.
- **Distribution**: Retail relationships with every major chain.
- **R&D**: PepsiCo Labs actively explores "away from home" dispensing innovation (Spire smart fountain system, connected vending).

### What PepsiCo Lacks (or Resists)

- **Incentive alignment**: Every home-dispensed serving cannibalizes a can sale. Canned soda has significantly higher margins than concentrate. PepsiCo's entire bottling and distribution infrastructure -- hundreds of thousands of jobs, billions in assets -- exists to move cans and bottles. A successful home dispensing product would undermine this.
- **SodaStream execution track record**: They paid $3.2B, wrote down over $1B, and the product still has the same fundamental physics problem (warm water, flat soda). Their one plumbed-in product (SodaStream Professional, launched 2020 for offices/campuses) was discontinued after ~4 years. There is no evidence PepsiCo knows how to build consumer hardware that works.
- **Organizational DNA**: PepsiCo is a CPG (consumer packaged goods) company. They make things that go on shelves. A plumbed-in kitchen appliance with microcontrollers, peristaltic pumps, solenoid valves, and refrigeration is a consumer electronics/appliance product. This is not their competency.
- **Channel conflict**: PepsiCo's bottling partners (independent and company-owned) would view a home dispensing product as a direct threat to their business. The channel conflict would be severe.
- **The Innovator's Dilemma**: PepsiCo cannot disrupt their own can business without destroying shareholder value in the process. This is the textbook case described by Clayton Christensen. The rational move for PepsiCo is always to protect can sales, not to cannibalize them.

### Signals to Watch

- Any PepsiCo announcement about a "connected home" or "home beverage" product beyond SodaStream.
- SodaStream launching a plumbed-in or refrigerated model (currently all countertop bottle-based; SodaStream Professional, their only plumbed-in product, was discontinued ~2024-2025).
- PepsiCo acquiring a home appliance company or carbonated water system maker.
- PepsiCo hiring for hardware/firmware engineering roles tied to consumer products.
- SodaStream partnering with an under-counter carbonator manufacturer.

### Current Signal Level: None

As of April 2026, there are zero public signals that PepsiCo is developing a plumbed-in home soda dispensing system. Their "Away from Home" innovation brief focuses on commercial fountain systems, vending, and QSR (quick-service restaurant) partnerships. SodaStream continues to iterate on countertop bottle carbonators. The syrup lineup is expanding, not contracting. Notably, PepsiCo has discontinued SodaStream Professional (~2024-2025) -- their only plumbed-in product, which served offices and campuses. They tried commercial plumbed-in dispensing for 4 years and walked away, which is the strongest negative signal yet for this category under PepsiCo.

## Three Summary Questions

### 1. Threat: ~10%

PepsiCo has all the assets to build a competing product (brand, syrup, SodaStream, capital) but faces crippling structural disincentives. The Innovator's Dilemma is real: every home dispense cannibalizes a high-margin can sale. Their SodaStream track record ($3.2B acquisition, $1B+ write-down, fundamental physics problem unresolved) and the discontinuation of SodaStream Professional (their only plumbed-in product, killed after 4 years) demonstrate they do not know how to execute in hardware and have actively retreated from plumbed-in dispensing. Their organizational DNA is CPG, not appliances. The most likely threat is not PepsiCo building a competitor -- it is PepsiCo discontinuing SodaStream concentrate (our supply chain). But the trend is toward more flavors, not fewer, and the syrup is sold through major retail channels that are difficult to unwind. The ~10% probability is driven almost entirely by the acquisition scenario: if the home soda category proves viable, PepsiCo is the most likely acquirer. They are unlikely to build it themselves.

### 2. Revenue and Units Estimate

- **PepsiCo Beverages North America (PBNA) segment revenue (FY2025):** $28.2 billion (all beverages, all formats).
- **Estimated PepsiCo CSD revenue, North America:** $14-16 billion/year.
- **Estimated PepsiCo canned/bottled retail CSD revenue, North America:** $9-11 billion/year.
- **Estimated PepsiCo CSD cans sold in the U.S.:** 15-17 billion cans per year.

### 3. Love / Hate

- **Love:** People love that a can of Pepsi requires zero thought -- open fridge, grab, pop, drink, same perfect product every time, anywhere.
- **Hate:** People hate hauling cases of cans from the store every week, the guilt of 730 empty cans per year per household, and the moment they open the fridge to find the shelf empty.
